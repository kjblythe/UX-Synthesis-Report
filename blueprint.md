## SECTION 2: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (Slides 5–7)

### Slide 5: Section Starter — “Executive Summary”

### Slide 6: Executive Summary

- **Format:** Matches A/B readout — context paragraph on the left, then 3 key findings as bolded headers with supporting detail.

**Context paragraph:**
“In April 2026, BI X conducted in-person moderated usability testing with 7 GLP-1 patients at a professional research facility to evaluate the interactive prototype ahead of the planned 2-month diary study pilot. Participants completed 6 structured tasks simulating a full day with the app — from morning exploration through medication logging, meal tracking, AI coach interaction, learning content, and evening reflection. The prototype was a fully interactive Lovable-built PWA with live AI integration. This study follows previously validated survey research, HCP interviews, and A/B testing.”

**Finding 1: Participants responded overwhelmingly positively to the app experience**
Post-session satisfaction averaged 6.6/7 across all measures. SEQ task ease scores averaged 6.4/7 — well above the industry benchmark of 5.5/7 established across 10,000+ users (MeasuringU). All 7 participants expressed willingness to use the app in their daily routine. Two participants — including the study’s most critical scorer — stated they would replace their current health app entirely. Core tasks (medication logging, meal logging) achieved 100% completion with near-perfect ease scores.

**Finding 2: AI-powered nutrition features drove the strongest engagement**
Photo-based meal logging emerged as a breakout feature — 6/7 participants chose it unprompted, with multiple expressing genuine surprise at the AI’s recognition accuracy. Participants consistently requested more depth in nutritional tracking, preferring specific calorie and macro data alongside qualitative nutrient descriptions.

**Finding 3: The app’s primary usability gap is visual styling, not information architecture**
Interactive elements (bottom navigation, action buttons, completion notifications) blend into the app’s polished dark aesthetic. This is a contrast and affordance issue, not a structural problem — confirmed by the participant with the lowest self-reported tech comfort (5/7) who navigated every feature intuitively on her first attempt.

## SECTION 5: TASK-BY-TASK RESULTS (Slides 20–23)

### Slide 20: Section Starter — “Detailed Results”

### Slide 21: What We Measured — Understanding SEQ

- **Purpose:** Educate stakeholders on the metric before showing results
- **Title:** “How We Measured Task Ease”
- **Content:**
  - **What is SEQ?** The Single Ease Question is a standardized post-task metric used across the UX industry. After each task, participants answer one question: “Overall, how easy or difficult was this task?” on a 7-point scale (1 = Very Difficult, 7 = Very Easy).
  - **Why it matters:** Because the SEQ is standardized, our results can be benchmarked against thousands of other products. It’s how we objectively compare our app’s usability to the broader market.
  - **The benchmark:** Research from MeasuringU across 400+ tasks and 10,000+ users established the industry average at **5.5/7** (50th percentile).
- **Visual:** A simple 1–7 scale graphic with zones marked:
  - 1–3: Difficult (red zone)
  - 4–5: Below average to average (yellow zone)
  - 5.5: Industry benchmark line (labeled “50th percentile — average across all products”)
  - 6–7: Easy / Excellent (green zone)
  - **Our app’s overall mean (6.4) marked prominently in the green zone**
- **Callout:** “Our app scored 6.4/7 across all tasks — well above the industry average of 5.5.”
- **Speaker notes:** “SEQ is the UX industry’s standard yardstick for task ease. Think of it like a credit score for usability — it lets us compare our product against any other product that’s been formally evaluated. The industry midpoint is 5.5. We scored 6.4. For context, research shows that a score of 5.9 corresponds to an 86% task completion rate. Most of our individual tasks scored above that.”

- **The primary usability finding.**
